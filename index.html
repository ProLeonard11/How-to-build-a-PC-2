How To Build a PC

How to build a PC

In this short manual, I will explain how I built my first PC and hopefully it will be a helpful guide.

Budget

The first thing to do when building a PC is set out a budget that you will stick to. You can give yourself a bit of leeway and add an extra £50 to £100 onto the budget, but just give yourself a rough estimate. A good starting PC price is about £700-1000. This will allow you to get some mid-tier quality parts without being overly expensive and still being reasonable.

Purpose of the PC and the Resolution

The next thing you want to consider is what kind of games or tasks you will be doing with the PC as well as what resolution you are going to be gaming in. There isn't really any point in buying a high-end graphics card and CPU if you are only going to be playing in 1080p. Similarly, if you want to also do other tasks that will require a higher-end PC such as 3D modeling or animation, then it would be useful to get parts that will accommodate and benefit that. Finally, you should also consider what types of games you are going to play. If you are only going to be playing graphically heavy games such as Fortnite or Warzone, then you don't need to spend as much on the CPU. Whereas if you plan on doing CPU-intensive games and tasks, you might want to opt for a less powerful graphics card in order to save money.

Parts list

Here is everything you will need in order to create a working computer.

  • CPU
  • GPU
  • Motherboard
  • RAM
  • PC Case
  • Storage
  • Power Supply

Optional Parts that will apply to higher-end PCs

  • CPU cooler (Most CPUs come with a stock cooler which will likely be fine for most people as you likely aren't going to be overclocking your CPU).
  • Thermal Paste (Most CPU coolers come with thermal paste inside the box, and some come with thermal paste pre-applied, such as stock coolers. Applying your own thermal paste can be beneficial as you will be able to cover the CPU with better quality thermal paste than the stock ones used.)
  • Extra case fans
  • Operating System Key (you can use a PC without, it will just have a watermark which is very insignificant)
  • PC case accessories such as custom cables and custom lighting

That is everything you need in order to make a PC, now we'll go over what components you should get in order to get the best experience possible for your budget.

CPU

Your CPU should be the very first thing you should consider when making a PC. You will either choose between two CPU companies: Intel and AMD.

There will be benefits to both of the brands but depending on your budget and the type of activities you will be doing on your PC, one will be the right one for you.

In this table, we will outline the benefits and drawbacks to each of the brands.

User Requirement AMD Intel
Price Cheaper More Expensive
Power Consumption Higher Power Consumption Lower Power Consumption
Cooling Factor Runs Hotter (on most models) Runs cooler (on most models)
Gaming Performance Faster Slower
Productivity AMD has worse performance Intel is better at productivity and multi-tasking
Reliability Less Reliable More Reliable

So, to summarize, Intel is generally better at multi-tasking and productivity but, AMD has more raw performance and will be better for gaming, as well as having better price to performance than Intel.

GPU

Much like CPUs there are two main brands that produce GPU those being Nvidia and AMD.

The GPU is likely going to be the most expensive part in your PC, this means you may want to prioritize this over other components. As stated before if you are going to be playing more graphic intensive games then spending more on your GPU will get you much better performance.

The best GPUs for Gaming

This table will give a brief outline of the best GPUs for each tier and price point, unlike CPUs there are a lot more options which will all be great options, you mainly want to find a graphics card that is correct for the resolution you want to play and a GPU inside of your budget.

So, to summarize AMD generally has cheaper GPUs and will offer better price to performance. But at the higher end of the scale, Nvidia is much better at 4k than AMD.

Motherboards

There are 3 major form factors of Motherboard: the standard ATX, the slightly smaller MicroATX and the even smaller Mini-ITX. The form factor you choose will greatly affect the slots and features on the board. Similarly, the size of the motherboard will determine the size of case you buy, for example, an ATX motherboard won't fit in a Mini-ITX case.

It doesn't really matter the brand of motherboard you get. The main thing to consider is the form factor and also the chipset that is compatible with the motherboard. For example, there are AM5 and AM4 motherboards that will only work with their corresponding chips. So, you must ensure you get the correct motherboard for your brand and model of CPU, and if you want to have a WIFI compatible motherboard.

RAM

When buying RAM you need to consider three things: Size of RAM, Speed of RAM, DDR4 Vs DDR5.

Size of RAM

The size of RAM usually ranges from 2GB to 64GB and even extending up to 128GB sometimes. The size of RAM will entirely depend on how much you are willing to spend on your PC, obviously more RAM will increase gaming and work performance but will be much more expensive

I would recommend that most people need at least 16GB of RAM if they want to play modern games. 32GB of RAM is probably a good amount for any Mid-range setup.

Speed of RAM

You can see the speed of your RAM by looking at the CAS latency, this number will usually be listed in the Specs of the product. The lower the number the faster the RAM

DDR4 Vs DDR5

There are two main types of RAM that are available, DDR4 and DDR5. DDR4 is the older and slower RAM but will be a lot cheaper than DDR5 this means that it will be fine for budget setups but probably wont be fast enough for Mid to higher tier builds. DDR5 will be faster at the downside of costing more because it is a lot more in demand.

PC Case

The PC case you buy will mainly have to support the size of your motherboard, so there is no point in buying a Mini-ITX case for an ATX motherboard because it wont fit. The PC case you choose will depend on 2 things: Price and Aesthetics. The type of case you choose won't impact performance but more expensive cases usually look better.

A good PC case for beginners is the Corsair 4000D

Storage

The capacity of storage and the type of storage are the two things you need to consider before purchasing. There is HDD, SSD, and NVMe drives. HDDs are quite old and out of date they are too slow for modern computers. SSD drives are good for modern low to mid range computers. The best type of storage is NVMe, as it is the fastest and also the smallest so its much easier to manage.

Power Supply

When making a PC you need to ensure that you get a power supply powerful enough for your setup, ensuring your power supply is adequate means you will get a better, smoother gaming performance. The best way to check how much power your PC needs is to look it up for your individual graphics card, this is because the GPU usually uses the most power. For example, the RTX 4070 has a recommended minimum PSU of 650W. Similarly, you want to find a PSU that has a good efficiency rating, this usually ranges from Bronze to Platinum, the higher the efficiency rating the better your PC will run.

The best way to find a good PSU is by looking at PSU tier lists, they are a very easy and efficient way to find a suitable PSU for your system.
